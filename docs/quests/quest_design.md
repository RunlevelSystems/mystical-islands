# Quest Design
## Quest Philosophy

Quest content in Mystical Isles should drive players outward into the world rather than trapping them in hub-only task loops.

Core principles:

- reward curiosity, preparation, and route planning
- make discovery feel valuable even before combat rewards are claimed
- connect quest outcomes to factions, regions, and world-state change
- use environmental storytelling and rumor chains to seed objectives
- support solo contribution while making full expeditions more effective
- let exploration reveal quests, not only the other way around

## Exploration Rewards

Exploration should provide multiple layers of value:

- reputation gains with island factions and expedition sponsors
- charts, clues, and route unlocks for future travel
- relic fragments, salvage, and rare crafting inputs
- access to hidden dungeons, vaults, and boss encounters
- world knowledge that changes available choices in later content

## Discovery-Driven Content Pillars

### Hidden Discoveries
- secret rooms, ruin consoles, coded journals, and forgotten shrines
- chained discoveries that expand after prior landmarks are found
- optional lore that rewards observant groups without blocking progression

### Dynamic World Events
- corruption blooms, pirate raids, ward failures, naval ambushes, and storm openings
- rotating events should alter travel routes and quest availability
- large events should create temporary cooperation across rival groups

### Faction Reputation
- quest decisions shift trust, prices, contracts, and access
- players should be able to specialize in a faction without locking all others forever
- some relic and exploration contracts should create moral or political tension

### Treasure Hunting
- treasure maps should require interpretation, not just waypoint following
- chart fragments, tide timing, and landmark recognition should matter
- naval hazards should make treasure runs feel distinct from land dungeons

### Relic Collection
- relic quests should combine combat, traversal, puzzle-solving, and lore context
- recovered objects should matter to factions beyond vendor value
- high-value relic chains can unlock technologies, ward repairs, or expedition routes

### Dungeon Discovery
- some dungeons must be found through exploration, rumors, or environmental changes
- repeated discovery can change dungeon state, occupants, or rewards over time
- unlocking a dungeon should feel like a world accomplishment, not a menu action

### Naval Quests and Island Expeditions
- sea travel should create missions around escort, interception, rescue, blockade, and navigation
- expeditions should feel like planned ventures with supplies, risks, and fallback choices
- island chains can be structured as multi-step voyages with resupply windows and branching outcomes

### World Bosses and Procedural Events
- world bosses should emerge from instability, migration, faction conflict, or relic misuse
- procedural events should remix known regions with fresh combinations of threats and rewards
- boss access can depend on route discovery, ward state, or community progress

## Quest Categories

### Main Story Quests
- reveal the widening scale of the archipelago's danger
- guide players from local survival toward faction-scale and world-scale stakes
- introduce The Fracturing indirectly at first, then more clearly through internal quest arcs

### Side Quests
- deepen regional identity through local rivalries, settlements, and folklore
- highlight ordinary people coping with extraordinary dangers
- provide repeatable relationship-building with towns and minor groups

### Exploration Quests
- begin through rumors, incomplete maps, strange landmarks, or found objects
- reward players for revisiting old regions with new tools or knowledge
- support branching outcomes depending on what was discovered first

### Guild Missions
- encourage coordination around supply runs, route security, ruin claims, and large hunts
- provide scalable objectives for different group sizes
- create rivalry through shared goals in contested regions

### Bounty Hunting
- target pirates, monsters, corrupted elites, and rogue expedition leaders
- combine tracking, identification, and optional capture-or-kill decisions
- use public bounty boards and hidden contracts for different tones of content

### Treasure Maps
- deliver navigation-driven gameplay through layered clues and environmental reading
- support counterfeit maps, faction interference, and race-style competition
- reward ship upgrades, relic caches, and rare cosmetic finds

### Ancient Relic Hunts
- send players into unstable ruins, sealed vaults, and contested dig sites
- tie rewards to artifact recovery, ward activation, and story escalation
- create repeatable late-game loops through rotating relic leads and anomaly zones
